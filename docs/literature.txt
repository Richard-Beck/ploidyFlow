1) Vindeløv et al. 1983

Standardization of High-Resolution Flow Cytometric DNA Analysis by the Simultaneous Use of Chicken and Trout Red Blood Cells as Internal Reference Standards

Relevant points for your project:

Their core premise is that DNA-content flow cytometry is a relative measurement, so a reference standard is required for meaningful DNA-content determination. They explicitly contrast external standards with internal standards, arguing that external standards are vulnerable to instrument drift and staining differences, whereas internal standards avoid those problems because they are stained with the sample.
They state that chicken red blood cells (CRBC) had already been widely used as an internal standard, but also identify a major limitation: CRBC are only about 35% of the human diploid DNA value, which makes ratios based on CRBC very sensitive to zero-level offset and other nonproportional shifts in channel position. This is directly relevant to your problem because it means “find the chicken peak and ratio everything to it” is not automatically robust.
They make a very concrete quantitative point about this sensitivity: in their schematic example, a 2-channel zero offset changes the DNA ratio by about 3.9% when using CRBC, versus only about 0.51% when using trout RBCs (TRBC). So if your workflow is trying to infer technical versus biological variability from chicken-anchored measurements, this paper is a warning that chicken alone may magnify technical artifacts.
Their solution was to use two internal standards simultaneously: CRBC plus TRBC. The conceptual value is not just “another peak,” but that two reference points make the DNA ratio effectively independent of the zero level. That is highly relevant methodologically even if you only have chicken in your data, because it frames reference extraction as an alignment/calibration problem, not just a single-peak detection problem.
They prepared a mixture of CRBC and TRBC, adjusted concentrations so the two peaks had roughly equal height in the histogram, froze aliquots, and added the standard mixture to the sample before staining. That gives you a concrete model for how internal standards were operationalized in high-resolution DNA-flow practice.
They report that these standard aliquots could be stored at −80°C for more than a year without change. That matters because it supports the idea that a reference standard can and should be treated as a reusable technical object rather than a sample-specific nuisance.
They added internal standards at an amount equal to 20% of the cells in the sample, with the CRBC:TRBC mixture tuned to a 4:7 ratio so the peaks had similar heights. For your purposes, the exact numbers may not transfer, but the general lesson is relevant: reference extraction is influenced not just by peak location but also by reference abundance and peak prominence.
For peak location, they did not use ad hoc local maxima. They explicitly say they fit Gaussians to each peak and used the fitted means for downstream calculations. That is very relevant for your workflow: this is direct support from the literature for model-based peak estimation rather than raw heuristic bump-picking.
Their reproducibility experiment was strong and relevant: they made 100 aliquots of one human mononuclear-cell sample mixed with both standards, then analyzed 4 aliquots per day on 25 days. That design is exactly the kind of thing that turns a reference peak into a technical QC object rather than just a normalization convenience.
The result of that experiment was that variation in the CRBC ratio was clearly larger than variation in the TRBC ratio, while the TRBC ratio and the corrected two-standard ratio were of similar magnitude. Their conclusion was that for DNA values near human diploid content, TRBC are more accurate and reproducible than CRBC alone, and that two standards are best for long-term comparability. This is one of the most important takeaways for you: chicken alone is not the ideal anchor if your target populations are near diploid/near-tetraploid human cells.
They explicitly examined other technical contributors and found no evidence that variation in staining time within 15 min to 3 h after staining, or variation across four different batches of staining solutions, explained the remaining variability. That is useful because it suggests where not to overfocus, at least within that prep system.
They looked at possible sex-related or batch-related differences in the standards. They found significant differences between male and female CRBC values and smaller but significant differences in TRBC values in their test set. Their operational conclusion was that a new batch of standards must be tested rigorously against the old batch before use, to avoid systematic error. This is highly relevant to your project because it means the chicken reference should not be treated as an abstract invariant; it is only invariant conditional on source/batch comparability.
They estimate that using the wrong sex/batch combination of standards could bias the determined DNA content of diploid human cells by about 1.3%. That gives you an actual order of magnitude for how much “technical invariance” can break even before you get to peak calling.
Their final framing is important for your exact use case: accurate DNA-flow determination near diploid is valuable as a simpler alternative to karyotype analysis for minor DNA abnormalities. That lines up well with your plan to use these well-characterized lines as a benchmark for what the flow assay can recover on its own.
Bottom line from Vindeløv for your project

The paper does not tell you how to identify the chicken peak from scratch in a complex mixed sample. But it gives three very important methodological lessons:

Internal standards should be modeled, not just eyeballed.
Chicken alone is a fragile anchor because of its low DNA content relative to human diploid.
Peak estimation should use fitted peak models and be evaluated through reproducibility across time/batches, not just per-sample detection.
2) Iversen & Laerum 1987

Trout and Salmon Erythrocytes and Human Leukocytes as Internal Standards for Ploidy Control in Flow Cytometry

Relevant points for your project:

Their motivation is directly relevant: during clinical ploidy work they found that the internal standard itself could be rather variable depending on the preparation procedure, so they systematically studied factors influencing the DNA stainability of trout/salmon erythrocytes and leukocytes. That is extremely close to your current problem, where you are trying to infer what variation in the reference peak is technical.
The single most important result in the paper is this: whether standard and test cells were mixed before or after enzymatic treatment and staining was critical. If they were mixed only after processing, the comparison could be badly distorted, with artifactual differences of at least 20% and major overestimation of aneuploidy. That is a huge methodological warning. If your samples were not co-processed with the chicken reference, then “same chicken across samples” becomes much shakier as an assumption.
Their actual prep was fairly explicit: ethanol-fixed cells, then pepsin treatment in acid, RNase, then DNA staining with ethidium bromide + mithramycin + MgCl2. For your purposes, the exact chemistry matters less than the principle: relative stainability is preparation-dependent, so reference identification and calibration cannot be divorced from sample-prep history.
They define DNA index using leukocytes as 1.00 or trout blood cells as 0.789, and CV as SD/mean percent. So the paper is operating squarely in the DNA-index calibration tradition, not just raw peak location. That is relevant because it supports thinking of your chicken peak as part of a calibration model.
Their reproducibility data are highly relevant. When TRBC and leukocytes were mixed after processing, the measured TRBC DNA index relative to leukocytes had a mean of about 0.702 ± 0.043. When they were mixed before pepsin treatment and staining, the mean was 0.789 ± 0.012, significantly different and much tighter. This is one of the strongest concrete pieces of evidence you have that co-processing matters enormously for reference reliability.
They repeatedly got single-peak histograms with good resolution (CV 1.3–3.0%) for the control populations over more than 12 months. That’s useful because it gives you a rough sense of what a high-quality reference peak looked like in practice.
They checked time from staining to analysis. In general it had minimal effect, but there were exceptions, especially for some late analyses of TRBC/endometrial mixes. On one day they saw no change up to 6 hours after staining, but some late measurements on other days were aberrant. So “time since staining” is not the main villain, but it is not totally ignorable either.
They tested linearity by varying instrument gain. Results were poor when cells were mixed after preparation and very good when mixed before preparation. Their conclusion was explicit: the most reliable results were obtained when standard and sample were mixed before preparation. That reinforces the same operational message from another angle.
They show that human leukocytes are not a neutral universal standard. When leukocytes were added after prep, they often differed significantly from diploid epithelial cells in benign and malignant tissues. When leukocytes were added before prep/staining, they could no longer be separated from diploid tissue cells. This is very relevant conceptually: reference extraction is not just about known genome content; it is also about stainability equivalence under the actual prep pipeline.
Using TRBC added before preparation, they found that diploid endometrial and ovarian cells, and the diploid fraction of aneuploid tumors, were measured as ~5–7% above human leukocytes. They interpret this as a staining-property difference rather than true DNA difference. This matters a lot for your project because it says that even a biologically correct reference can imply a biased DNA index if the reference and the biological cells stain differently.
They also used morphology/histology to check what was actually in the samples. That is a reminder that if your automated flow pipeline starts making strong claims about subpopulations, external morphology can still matter as validation.
In ascites/tumor comparisons, they found that tumor-cell DNA indices in ascites were consistently higher than in the corresponding tumor tissue when diploid cells in each sample were used as the reference. They interpret this as additional support that tissue epithelial nuclei and admixed control/support cells can have different stainability from leukocyte DNA. Again, the key point for you is: reference peaks and sample peaks do not necessarily scale identically under the assay chemistry.
Their general conclusion is blunt and directly useful for your workflow: DNA index and ploidy aberrations are reliable only when sample and reference cells are mixed before enzymatic treatment and staining.
Bottom line from Iversen for your project

This paper is probably the most important cautionary one of the two. Its main messages are:

Reference extraction is inseparable from preparation chemistry.
Co-processing of reference and sample is critical.
Apparent ploidy shifts can easily be artifacts of differential stainability, even when the reference is “known.”
